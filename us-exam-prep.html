Proven Study Strategies

Techniques backed by cognitive science to help you prepare effectively for any exam.

⏲

Spaced Repetition

Review material at increasing intervals (1 day, 3 days, 1 week, 2 weeks, 1 month). This technique, pioneered by psychologist Hermann Ebbinghaus, fights the forgetting curve by reinforcing neural pathways exactly when recall is about to fade. Use tools like Anki or Quizlet to automate review scheduling.

📖

Active Recall

Close the book and test yourself. Don't just re-read notes — force your brain to retrieve information. Write down everything you remember about a topic, then check against your materials. Research shows active recall is 2-3x more effective than passive review for long-term retention.

🏆

Practice Tests

Take full-length timed practice exams under real test conditions. Simulate the actual testing environment: same time limits, no distractions, no phone. Review every mistake thoroughly — each wrong answer is a learning opportunity. Aim for at least 3-5 full practice tests before exam day.

⏰

Pomodoro Technique

Study in focused 25-minute blocks, followed by 5-minute breaks. After 4 cycles, take a 15-30 minute break. This method, developed by Francesco Cirillo, prevents burnout and maintains concentration. Use a timer app to track your sessions and build consistency.

📝

The Feynman Technique

Teach the concept to someone else in simple terms, as if explaining to a 12-year-old. Nobel laureate Richard Feynman believed that you don't truly understand something until you can explain it simply. If you struggle to explain it, you've found a knowledge gap to fill.

🏃

Physical & Mental Wellness

Sleep 7-9 hours — especially the night before. Exercise 3 times per week to boost BDNF, a protein that enhances learning. Eat brain-healthy foods (blueberries, nuts, fatty fish). Meditate for 10 minutes daily to reduce test anxiety. Your brain performs best when your body is well cared for.

💡

Interleaving

Mix up subjects and problem types during study sessions instead of blocking them. For example, if studying for the GRE, switch between verbal, quantitative, and writing practice within one session. This approach builds cognitive flexibility and improves your ability to identify which strategy to apply.

🛠

Error Analysis Log

Keep a dedicated notebook where you record every mistake from practice problems. For each error, write: the correct answer, why you got it wrong, and what you'll do differently next time. Review this log weekly — pattern recognition in your errors is the fastest path to improvement.

📅

General Preparation Timeline

A roadmap that applies to most standardized exams. Adjust based on your target test.

6 Months Before — Diagnostic & Planning

Take a full-length diagnostic test to identify strengths and weaknesses. Research target scores for your schools or licensing boards. Create a study schedule with weekly goals. Gather official prep materials and register for your test date.

4 Months Before — Content Review

Systematically review all content areas, focusing heavily on your weak spots. Spend 60-70% of time on weaker areas and 30-40% on reinforcement of strengths. Complete chapter-by-chapter exercises in prep books. Begin building your error analysis log.

2 Months Before — Practice Phase

Transition from content review to timed practice. Take one full practice test every 1-2 weeks under real conditions. Analyze results thoroughly and adjust your study plan. Join a study group or hire a tutor for difficult areas.

3 Weeks Before — Intensive Drilling

Focus on timing strategies and test-day pacing. Take your final practice tests. Review all error logs and note patterns. Memorize key formulas, vocabulary, or frameworks. Fine-tune your test-taking strategy (order of sections, time allocation).

1 Week Before — Final Review & Rest

Light review only — no new material. Review summary sheets, flashcards, and your error log. Prepare logistics: ID, admission ticket, directions to test center, approved calculator, snacks. Prioritize sleep: 8+ hours every night. Visualize success with positive mental rehearsal.

Test Day — Execute Your Plan

Eat a protein-rich breakfast. Arrive 30 minutes early. Use the restroom before the exam begins. Read every question carefully. Trust your preparation. Manage time: if stuck, mark and return later. Take deep breaths between sections. You've prepared — now perform.
